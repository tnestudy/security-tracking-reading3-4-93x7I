Read the following article from a website.
A recent surge of high-profile data breaches has prompted renewed interest in cybersecurity. At issue is the security and confidentiality of thousands of terabytes of business and government data. Some experts are now warning that, unless drastic steps are taken to strengthen cybersecurity, the entire process of digitalization could be threatened.

"The past few months have been really unnerving for a lot of decision-makers. People read the news and they realize that breaches could happen to their company, often jeopardizing years of work, if not their organization's very survival," said Mona Jackman, a federal government policy analyst. "It's gotten to the point where people are questioning the whole digital revolution. I can't really say I blame them. When you look at the scale of the leaks and the strength of the organizations that were hacked, you have to feel vulnerable. If you are a small business owner who depends on proprietary information, isn't it better to leave everything offline and stick with pen and paper methods?"

"Those who say that hacking could somehow harm the IT industry don't understand the long-term technological forces that are at work. Solutions are being found as more cash is infused into the system," opined Cassandra Black, an IT industry analyst. "Security consultants make thousands of dollars installing firewalls, authentication software, and cyberactivity monitoring systems. Cybersecurity companies have been flourishing; these high-profile hacking cases are like free publicity for them."

However, not everyone believes that more costly, sophisticated systems are the answer to modern cybersecurity threats. "When dealing with hackers, the best tool we have in our arsenal is education," weighs in Mark Levinstein, Chief Information Officer at a large Canadian engineering consulting firm. "Most of these large leaks were the results of very simple phishing scams, whereby hackers were unwittingly given network access by employees. I don't worry for the IT industry, because these are basic issues that can be resolved at a low cost, but we need proper training and coordination across the sector."
